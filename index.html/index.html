Systemic Responsibility Allocation Model

A diagnostic framework for evaluating how responsibility, risk, and stability are distributed across organizational roles.

This prototype does not ask only who is at fault. It asks how a role is structurally positioned: how much leverage it has, how large its consequences may be, how foreseeable those consequences are, and whether the role can be replaced, reversed, or redistributed.

The goal is to identify responsibility concentration, systemic risk exposure, and structural fragility before harm escalates.

Role Structure Inputs

Each variable below is visible and explained. Select the description that best matches the role you want to assess.

Structural Input

Capacity

Capacity refers to how much structural leverage a role has over outcomes.

In this model, higher capacity means a role can shape more consequences, so it tends to increase responsibility concentration.

Structural Input

Impact

Impact refers to the likely scale and depth of consequences associated with the role.

Higher impact increases both the ethical importance of the role and the degree of systemic risk if decisions fail.

Structural Input

Foreseeability

Foreseeability refers to how far the consequences of this role can be reasonably anticipated.

This variable matters especially for Ex Ante responsibility: the more predictable the consequences, the stronger the preventive obligation.

Structural Input

Exit Conditions

Exit refers to whether influence from this role can be reduced, replaced, reversed, or redistributed.

Weak exit conditions make responsibility accumulate. Strong exit conditions reduce fragility and improve structural stability.
