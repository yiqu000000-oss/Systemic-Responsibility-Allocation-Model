Project Overview

This project explores how responsibility is distributed in systems where individuals possess unequal levels of capacity, foresight, and exit options.

Many ethical frameworks focus on individual wrongdoing. However, complex organizations often create situations where responsibility becomes structurally uneven rather than purely personal.

Systems frequently punish visible violations while overlooking preventive responsibility.

The research began with a case study from Journey to the West, particularly the episode of the White Skeleton Demon. In this story, Sun Wukong anticipates danger before harm occurs, while Tripitaka enforces moral rules based primarily on visible conduct.

This tension illustrates a deeper ethical problem: institutions often reward compliance with visible rules while failing to recognize roles that bear the burden of foresight and preventive action.

Based on this observation, the project develops a conceptual framework for analyzing how organizational roles concentrate responsibility and systemic risk.

The interactive prototype on the main page is an early experiment designed to visualize this framework. It is not a finalized analytical tool, but a way to explore how different role structures distribute responsibility, foreseeability, and systemic risk.

The long-term goal of the project is to develop a structured approach for diagnosing responsibility distribution in organizations and producing analytical reports in the field of business and institutional ethics.
